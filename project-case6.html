EV Charging Network

01 — Challenge

The Problem

Electric vehicle adoption is accelerating across the United States, but EV charging infrastructure is unevenly distributed. Expanding charging networks efficiently requires understanding which counties are genuinely ready — not just large or prosperous — and which are still developing the conditions that make investment viable.

  • No data-driven framework existed to segment counties by EV market readiness
  • Manual analysis of 38 counties across 15+ variables was too complex for clear decision-making
  • Existing charging networks were concentrated in 10 counties, leaving 28 unserved
  • Business stakeholders needed a prioritised expansion list, not just raw data
  • Different counties had fundamentally different growth profiles — a single strategy would not work across all 28 targets

Core question: Which counties share similar EV market characteristics — and what does each group tell us about the right expansion strategy?

02 — Data

Dataset & Feature Engineering

The dataset covered 38 U.S. counties for 2024, with 17 raw variables spanning demographics, infrastructure, energy mix, and EV market metrics. Zero missing values. Two ratio features were engineered to improve analytical signal.

Engineered Features

  • pop_density = population ÷ land_area_sqmi — separates dense urban cores from sprawling metro areas
  • ev_per_capita = ev_registrations ÷ population — captures EV market intensity, not just raw volume

Feature Selection Decision: Removing Composite Scores

Four variables in the dataset were analyst-constructed composites rather than raw measurements: charging_demand_index, infrastructure_readiness, market_potential_score, and economic_viability. These were excluded from the final clustering for two principled reasons:

  • Severe multicollinearity. Pairwise correlations among the composites reached 0.95–0.98 (e.g. market_potential_score vs. ev_registrations at 0.98). Leaving them in would allow the same underlying signal to vote 3–4 times in the Euclidean distance calculation, inflating its influence disproportionately.
  • Target leakage risk. All four composites correlate strongly with the validation label has_charging_network (0.65–0.78). Since the methodology for constructing these scores was not disclosed, including them could mean the clusters simply rediscover information already baked into the label.

The final feature set used 11 raw and engineered variables, giving a cleaner, more interpretable clustering solution.

03 — Methodology

Analytical Approach

Step 1 — Standardisation

All 11 features were standardised using StandardScaler (mean=0, std=1) to ensure no single variable dominated distance calculations due to scale differences (e.g. population in millions vs. ev_adoption_rate as a decimal).

Step 2 — PCA Dimensionality Reduction

Principal Component Analysis reduced the 11-feature matrix to 2 components, retaining 81.5% of total variance. With only 38 data points, parsimony was critical — each additional component adds degrees of freedom for noise to exploit.

  • PC1 (57.8%) — "EV Market Maturity": All major loadings positive. Heaviest contributors: infrastructure_readiness (0.323), charging_demand_index (0.322), market_potential_score (0.309), ev_registrations (0.297), avg_gas_price (0.286). High-PC1 counties are affluent, EV-ready, with strong charging demand.
  • PC2 (23.8%) — "Scale vs Density": Positive loadings on land_area_sqmi (0.396), population (0.391), highway_miles (0.302). Negative on ev_adoption_rate (−0.363), pop_density (−0.087). High-PC2 = large sprawling metros (LA, Cook). Low-PC2 = dense urban cores (SF, Marin) with high per-capita EV.

Step 3 — K Selection

Three methods were used to determine the optimal number of clusters:

  • Elbow method (SSE): The curve kinks clearly at k=3 — from 244.7 (k=2) to 152.9 (k=3), then diminishing returns
  • Silhouette analysis: k=3 scores 0.508. While k=7 peaks marginally at 0.512, the 0.004 difference is negligible and k=7 with n=38 produces near-singleton clusters that are not business-actionable
  • Sample-size heuristic: √(38/2) ≈ 4 — supports 3–4 clusters

Decision: k=3. Interpretability and sample-size discipline outweigh a 0.004 silhouette gap at k=7. Three clusters produce three distinct, business-meaningful county segments.

Step 4 — Stability Validation

K-Means was re-run across 8 different random seeds. The Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) across all runs: mean = 1.000, min = 1.000, max = 1.000. This is a perfectly stable solution — every initialisation produces identical cluster assignments.

Cluster 0 — Established Hubs

The highest EV market maturity. High income ($100,700 median), 3% EV adoption rate, 60% already have networks. These are affluent, EV-progressive counties in coastal metros. Counties: Santa Clara, San Francisco, King (WA), Alameda, Marin, Boulder (CO), Fairfax (VA), Montgomery (MD), Middlesex (MA), Suffolk (MA).

Cluster 1 — Emerging Markets

The largest and most diverse group. Lower income ($62,636), 1% EV adoption, only 1 of 22 has a network. These markets are growing but not yet EV-mature. High variance within this cluster reflects a wide range of development stages. Strong expansion opportunity with the right market-entry timing.

Cluster 2 — Mega-Metros

Defined not by EV maturity but by sheer scale — average population over 5.1M, the largest counties by land area and highway miles. Despite mid-range income and only 1% EV adoption, 3 of 6 already have networks (LA, Orange, San Diego). The challenge here is scale of infrastructure, not market readiness.

05 — Insights

Key Insights & Business Interpretation

  1. Cluster 0 counties are the most investable — but also the most competitive. With 60% already having networks, the remaining 4 (Fairfax, Montgomery, Middlesex, Suffolk) represent high-probability, lower-risk expansion targets in already-proven markets. First-mover advantage is diminishing in these areas.
  2. Cluster 2 (Mega-Metros) requires a different strategy entirely. The 3 network-free mega-metros (Cook County/Chicago, Harris County/Houston, Maricopa County/Phoenix) have massive demand potential but require large-scale, phased infrastructure investment rather than the targeted deployments suited to Cluster 0.
  3. Cluster 1 is where strategic selection matters most. With 21 of 22 counties having no network, this cluster contains the most expansion opportunities — but also the most heterogeneity. Sub-segmenting or applying the priority scoring model within Cluster 1 is essential for investment discipline.
  4. EV adoption rate is the strongest real-world differentiator. The gap between Cluster 0 (3.0%) and Clusters 1 & 2 (1.0%) is the clearest signal of market readiness — and it aligns with the high correlations seen in the feature analysis.
  5. The perfectly stable solution (ARI=1.000) validates the clustering structure. This is not a fragile result sensitive to initialisation — the three segments are genuinely robust in the data.
06 — Recommendations

Expansion Priority Rankings

The 28 counties without networks were ranked using a transparent weighted priority score combining five business-relevant factors:

  • 30% — Market Potential Score
  • 25% — Infrastructure Readiness
  • 20% — Charging Demand Index (rescaled)
  • 15% — Economic Viability
  • 10% — Renewable Energy % (ESG alignment)
